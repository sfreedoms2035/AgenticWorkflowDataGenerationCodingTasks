Abstract
Self-driving cars have tremendous potential to be safer than human drivers, but are
complex systems for which evaluating safety is challenging: using a statistical ap-
proach requires self-driving cars to have clocked on the order of billions of miles of
driving to present convincing evidence. Thus there is potential in exploring a new
design architecture for self-driving cars in which a small, trusted module of code co-
operates with the main controller to ensure safety while being easily verifiable; we
call this the safety Interlock. This thesis focuses on the scenario of an ego car driving
in a single, straight lane behind a lead car that may suddenly brake. We first propose
and prove, using formal verification, an algorithm for Interlock to prevent collision
by maintaining a safe separation distance that allows the ego car to stop in time. We
then present a simulation program developed using the Processing programming lan-
guage, which provides visual confirmation of the efficacy of the Interlock algorithm,
and is designed to be extensible to more complex road scenarios.

    Chapter 1

    Introduction


1.1 Motivation

There have been multiple high-profile accidents in recent years involving self-driving
cars, including a few that have resulted in fatalities. While most of these fatalities
were the drivers of these cars, the first pedestrian fatality has already occurred in
2018 [1], providing a stark reminder that the safety of self-driving cars is an issue
that affects all groups of road users. At the same time, given the potential of self-
driving cars to drive more safely than human drivers and reduce accident rates [2],
it would be unfortunate if negative public perception and unaddressed safety issues
prevent the realization of this potential. Multiple challenges currently stand in the
way: self-driving cars are very complex systems prone to failure in various aspects,
and lack the human intuition and contextual understanding required in many road
scenarios. A major challenge is to ensure safety without being too conservative.
   In terms of verifying safety, a statistical data-driven method is too expensive,
requiring an infeasible thirty billion miles to be driven in order to prove that self-
driving cars are three orders of magnitude safer than human drivers [3]. A critical
metric for evaluating safety is the number of fatalities per 100,000,000 (100 million)
miles driven. In 2016, this figure was 1.18 fatalities for 100 million miles driven by
human drivers [4]. Self-driving cars have yet to drive enough distance to provide a
convincing data point for this metric: as of February 2018, industry leader Waymo

        10

has covered 5 million miles with its autonomous vehicles, and this was with a human
driver on board to monitor and override the vehicle’s autonomous mode to improve
safety [5].


1.2 The Broader Context

This thesis is part of a larger project to design a new architecture for self-driving
cars. It began with what we termed a safety Interlock, which was envisioned as a
small module of code separate from most other system components (e.g. GPS and
vision) and hence less affected by failures in these other aspects. The Interlock should
interact cohesively with the existing controller, and be able to veto instructions sent
to the actuators or send its own instructions, in order to keep the car safe in a variety
of road scenarios.
   As a small, modular piece of code, the Interlock can be independently verified
to maintain safety in different scenarios. This avoids the issue present in the sta-
tistical data-driven approach that requires driving on the order of billions of miles
to convincingly evaluate the safety of a self-driving car system. Formal methods—
techniques and tools based in mathematics—can be used to specify and verify such
an Interlock system [6]; they involve a process of formulating specifications and then
verifying that these specifications are fulfilled by a proposed design. One class of
computational tools used in formal methods are satisfiability modulo theories (SMT)
solvers [7], and two such SMT solvers—Microsoft’s Z3 Theorem Prover [8] and the
open-source dReal [9]—were used in this project.
   Finally, the team decided that simulation programs and physical demonstrations
would prove helpful in the safety argument. For the latter, the team is programming
RC cars with the Interlock algorithm. This thesis presents a simulation program
developed for this purpose, and also serves to consolidate important discussions and
core findings in the early stages of the project, notably the ‘single lane, two cars’ road
scenario elaborated on in the next section.



    11

1.3 Contributions

   ∙ Formal verification of a safe separation distance in the ‘single lane, two cars’
      scenario. This is the case in which the self-driving car is driving behind another
      car that may brake at any moment. We use formal techniques to verify the
      minimum distance the self-driving car should maintain to the car in front so
      that it can stop in time to avoid collision should the car in front suddenly
      decelerate.

   ∙ A simulator to model a range of road scenarios. Designed to provide visual
      feedback on the behavior of all cars at all times in the simulation of a road
      scenario, this simulator can catch unexpected behaviors that are not evident
      through formal verification.

   ∙ Exploration of the “jittering” issue and how to resolve it. Jittering is one such
      unexpected behavior captured by the simulator, in which the self-driving car
      rapidly cycles between acceleration and deceleration as it repeatedly violates and
      regains safe separation distance to the car in front. We discuss ways of solving
      this and demonstrate a working strategy that we verified using the simulator.

    Chapter 2

    The Single Lane, Two Cars Scenario



This section analyzes the case where the ego car is driving behind another car—
the lead car—in a single, straight lane. In the real world, this best models suburban
driving, such as on a one-way road or on a two-lane road with traffic going in opposite
directions in either lane. Without the option of changing lanes, the ego car needs to
constantly maintain a safe separation distance from the lead car to avoid collision if
the latter suddenly stops. This section presents the derivation of this safe separation
distance from the velocity and acceleration properties of both cars. It proposes that
the Interlock intervene when the distance between both cars is less than the safe
separation distance, and provides a formal verification that this strategy indeed keeps
the ego car safe.
   A complication is that the Interlock runs periodically and so is limited by its cycle
time in how frequently it can check whether the safe separation distance has been
violated. Furthermore, the sensors from which the Interlock obtains environmental
information—including the distance to the lead car—and the actuators that actually
turn the instructions from the Interlock into physical actions of the ego car have their
own delays as well. This means that in a particular cycle, the Interlock might be
using stale sensor data to determine the car’s next course of action; then when it
decides this next action, it has to account for the delay in propagating instructions to
the actuators and in the actuators manifesting these instructions. These delays and
cycle times are addressed in Section 2.5.

maximum deceleration.

2.6 Interlock Algorithm for Safe Separation

Now we present an algorithm for the ego car to maintain safe separation SS when
driving behind a lead car in a single, straight lane. For a given Interlock cycle which
affects its own actuation point until the actuation point of the next Interlock cycle:

   ∙ If SS is satisfied based on the currently available sensor readings, do not inter-
      vene.

   ∙ If SS is not satisfied, apply maximum deceleration 𝐷.

   We make the claim that if ES is satisfied at one actuation point, then it is satisfied
continuously until the next actuation point, and prove it so:

   ∙ If the Interlock cycle corresponding to this actuation point decided to intervene,
      then we have maximum deceleration 𝐷 until the next actuation point. By
      Theorem 2.4.2, ES holds continuously until the next actuation point.

   ∙ If the Interlock cycle corresponding to this actuation point decided not to inter-
      vene, then SS holds at the time of sensor reading, so by Lemma 2.5.1, ES holds
      continuously until the next actuation point, which occurs at time ≤ 𝑒 after the
      sensor reading.

Corollary 2.6.0.1 If ES is satisfied at the first actuation point of the Interlock cycle,
then ES holds all the time (and there is no collision after the first actuation point).

   As a final step to round off this chapter, we account for our initial treatment of
both cars as points; it is trivial to factor in the car lengths, and we simply add half
the length of the ego car and half the length of the lead car to the necessary safe
separation distances calculated in the above sections.

    The Simulator



In the previous chapter we established conditions for a safe separation distance that
an ego car driving on a single, straight lane needs to maintain from a lead car driving
ahead, in order to avoid collision should the latter decelerate to a stop. In each cycle,
the Interlock checks if the safe separation distance to the lead car is violated based
on the currently available sensor readings, and applies maximum deceleration in the
ego car if this distance is violated.
   We have developed a simulator to visualize a self-driving car acting under the con-
trol of the Interlock for the ‘single lane, two cars’ scenario described in the previous
chapter. This provides a form of visual confirmation, in addition to the formal verifi-
cation presented in the previous chapter, that the Interlock will keep the car safe in
that scenario. Furthermore, the simulator allows us to catch any unexpected behavior
that might not be evident from formal verification and verification with SMT solvers
like dReal. The usefulness of this will become apparent at the end of this chapter.
   Because this simulator began with the goal of helping the team towards a verified
implementation of an Interlock for different road scenarios, it was designed to include
the versatility to represent a variety of road scenarios, such as roads with multiple
lanes of traffic, obscured intersections, and the inclusion of pedestrians. The potential
of the simulator to verify the Interlock in all these different scenarios is explored in
Chapter 5.

3.3.1 Entry Point of Program

car_sim.pde is the main file of the simulation sketch. It renders what the user sees
in the window using Processing’s 𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑢𝑝() API call [15]. 𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑢𝑝() is run once when the
sketch starts and never again after its initial execution, and each sketch can have only
one 𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑢𝑝() function. It is used to define initial environment properties such as screen
size, and for the simulator it also sets up UI/UX components like the play/pause
button in the lower right.
   The simulator enables the UI/UX features described in Subsection 3.2.1 by using
the 𝑚𝑜𝑢𝑠𝑒𝑃 𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑑(), 𝑘𝑒𝑦𝑃 𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑑() and 𝑘𝑒𝑦𝑅𝑒𝑙𝑒𝑎𝑠𝑒𝑑() API calls in Processing to
capture user input from the mouse and keyboard.
   It parses arguments that are passed after −−run when the simulation is launched
from the command line (see previous section), and uses those arguments to populate
(i) a list of Road objects with the user-defined position, length, orientation, number
of lanes, and lane width, and (ii) a list of Car objects initialized at the user-defined
positions on their respective roads and with user-specified initial velocities and accel-
erations. It also creates a World object that has knowledge of all the Cars in the
simulation and tracks their positions at every timestep. The following subsections
further describe these objects.
   The program achieves animation using the 𝑑𝑟𝑎𝑤() API call [16], which continu-
ously executes the lines of code within the function to regenerate what the user sees in
the window, at a certain frame rate (which can be specified; here we use default frame
rate in Processing). As with 𝑠𝑒𝑡𝑢𝑝(), each sketch can only have one 𝑑𝑟𝑎𝑤() function.
Moreover, it must exist for the code to run continuously and to process events such
as 𝑚𝑜𝑢𝑠𝑒𝑃 𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑠𝑒𝑑() that enable the UI/UX features. The simulator is implemented
such that every time 𝑑𝑟𝑎𝑤() runs to regenerate the window, it (i) renders the static
UI/UX components and Roads, and (ii) redraws the moving Cars in their latest
positions on the screen.

   pixels_per_meter, as its names suggests, dictates how many pixels represent a
meter in the world of our simulation. It allows us to think in terms of meters rather
than pixels when specifying dimensions and position for the Road and Car objects,

the Car objects; we update the position of each Car in the next frame by computing
its displacement from its current position in the next 1/90.0 seconds.

3.3.2 The World Object

A simulation has only one World object; it represents the state of the world of
our simulation. car_sim.pde passes the list of all Cars to this object when it is
first initialized, and the World acts as a layer in the program that (i) continuously
tracks the position of every Car in the simulation, and (ii) moves each Car to its
new position in the next frame if the animation is unpaused. It does the latter
by tracking a boolean variable called halt that indicates whether the animation is
currently paused. If so, World does not trigger any update to the positions of the
Cars that it tracks, so a frozen view of this world is returned until the user resumes
the animation.
   The World object has the following two-dimensional integer array variable

int [ ] [ ] occupancy_grid



    37

that represents the global occupancy grid. The dimensions of the 2D array correspond
to the size of the display window measured in pixels, and a 0 in a cell indicates that
the pixel at the same location on the screen is unoccupied. Where there is a car, the
pixels that the car occupies correspond to 1’s in the cells representing these pixels in
the occupancy_grid array.
   If the animation is not paused, then World checks, for each Car, whether its
current position has any overlap with occupied pixels in the occupancy grid—this
constitutes a collision—and updates the position of the car in the next frame by
adding its displacement in the next 𝑠𝑒𝑐𝑜𝑛𝑑𝑠_𝑝𝑒𝑟_𝑓 𝑟𝑎𝑚𝑒. Subsection 3.3.4 discus-
sions collision checking in more detail.

3.3.3 The Car Object

The Car object is the representation of a car in the world of our simulator, and is
the program layer that:

   ∙ implements methods to initialize the position, velocity, and acceleration of the
      car given user input passed to car_sim.pde from the command line

   ∙ implements methods to allow the user to manually steer the car and acceler-
      ate/decelerate using the keyboard while the animation is running

   ∙ renders visual feedback of the data gathered by the car’s sensors, such as the
      range of the car’s LiDAR represented as a gray circle in the demo

   ∙ performs collision checking by tracking an array of PVectors (a class in Pro-
      cessing that describes a Euclidean/geometric vector [17]) in the variable cur-
      rent_occupancy which represents the coordinates occupied by this car, and
      implementing a check_collision() method (see Subsection 3.3.4)

   ∙ implements the Interlock algorithm that acts in the ego car to keep it safe (see
      Section 3.4)

   This Car object represents the most general type of car and implements the most
general methods to adequately model a real car in the road scenarios we want to

3.5 Uncovering the Jittering Problem

The “jittering” problem became apparent from observing the behavior of the ego car
in multiple simulations as Interlock kicks in to maintain safe separation distance to
the lead car. It is clearer in animation, but we do our best in Figure 3-8 to show what
we mean by “jittering”: the ego car continuously alternates between decelerating
and accelerating as it re-establishes safe separation distance. This is evident from

        46

observing the black outline repeatedly and rapidly appearing and disappearing around
the ego car.
   Jittering is not conducive for driver comfort, and worse, poses a safety concern.
The next chapter explores ways of tackling this problem.
   More broadly, this is an example of issues we hope to catch using the simulator
that may not be apparent in formal verification, owing to the visual and interactive
nature of the simulator. It also raises the question of what “keeping the car safe”
should look like: is preventing collision the only or the most important aspect of
safety? Is it safer to brake at maximum deceleration in order to completely avoid
a collision, or to brake with a weaker deceleration that doesn’t avoid collision but
reduces the impact of collision while being more comfortable for the passenger? Even
in the single lane scenario, in the real world the ego car would have some leeway
to swerve to avoid rear-ending the lead car head-on—is it then safer for the ego car
to brake with weaker deceleration but also swerve slightly? The simulator currently

3.6 Limitations of the Simulator

   ∙ Many values are hard-coded in the current implementation of the simulator,
      including: the magnitude of acceleration/deceleration applied to the ego car
      when the user presses the ‘w’/‘s’ keys, the dimensions (length and width) of
      the cars, and the maximum deceleration of the cars. We decided that these
      factors did not need to be very customizable for the current purposes of the
      simulator, and have chosen sensible values for them. Nevertheless, it is possible
      to (i) modify how the simulator parses command line arguments to allow these
      variables to be customized, or (ii) implement a front-end UI to ingest user input
      to customize these variables.

   ∙ In Processing, the simulation can be launched from the terminal, allowing us
      to design to a highly customizable degree how command line arguments are
      parsed to generate creative road scenarios in the simulator. However, this loses
      the versatility of Processing.js which can be run in a browser without needing
      anything to be installed. A way to remedy this is to implement front-end
      components to take in user input, but this will require some work.

   ∙ The present implementation of collision-checking means that every time draw()
      runs, we call check_collision() for every Car in the World. For each Car,
      check_collision() first loops over all the PVectors representing the space this
      Car previously occupied in the World and then updates the World’s knowl-
      edge of the current space occupied by this Car, checking for collision during
      this process. This is not the most computationally efficient algorithm, and there
      is an unverified risk that this could slow the simulation down when more cars
      are added. We have not observed this to be a problem for this scenario that
      involves just two cars, nor for some other experimental scenarios we have simu-
      lated that involved more (but still fewer than ten) cars, but it might be of value
      to redesign the collision-checking algorithm to be more efficient.

   ∙ The present implementation of Lidar is not very extensible to scenarios in-

        49

  volving more than one car, since we assume that the Car_Info at the front
  of list of Car_Infos returned by the ego car’s Lidar scan represents the lead
  car. This works for the ‘single lane, two cars’ scenario, but results in errors
  when there are multiple other cars and the relative positions of the other cars
  to the ego car can change. A more sound method for determining which of the
  other cars is currently closest to the ego car (whether directly ahead or in any
  direction around the ego car) will be necessary for analyzing more complicated
  road scenarios in future, such as those involving lane changing which we shall
  explore in Chapter 5.

∙ The simulator works well as a visual aid for animating some user-created road
  scenario given certain initial conditions. It works less well when we attempt
  to extend it to any sort of more statistical function. For instance, it is not
  easy to automate the running of massive numbers of simulations of the ‘single
  lane, two cars’ scenario with randomly generated initial velocity and acceler-
  ation conditions to verify that the Interlock algorithm indeed guarantees no
  collision. Processing will launch a window for every simulation and run every
  simulation until a collision occurs (or, if no collision occurs, we need to impose
  some other stopping condition that indicates that the ego car remained safe for
  this simulation, such as exiting the simulation once the cars have traveled a
  certain distance). We could improve the computational efficiency by avoiding
  drawing the roads and cars if we just want the aggregate result of many sim-
  ulations, but clearly this illustrates that our simulator is geared more towards
  visual verification.

    Chapter 4

    Addressing Jittering



At the end of the previous chapter, we surfaced the problem of jittering in the ‘single
lane, two cars’ scenario. Jittering occurs because the ego car brakes upon getting too
close to the lead car, but then starts accepting original acceleration inputs (either
from the initial acceleration argument in the command to launch the simulation,
or from user input through keyboard commands while the animation is running)
immediately after regaining safe separation distance. This causes it to quickly violate
the safe separation distance again in the next few timesteps/frames, which triggers
the Interlock to cause the car to brake.
   This chapter explores options to prevent this unstable and potentially unsafe be-
havior.


    4.1   Initial Exploration

    Since jittering arises due to the ego car applying positive acceleration “too soon” after
    the Interlock has just kicked in to decelerate the car, we reason that a logical initial
    approach is to prevent the ego car from accepting any acceleration inputs from the
    moment the Interlock is triggered, until a “safe point”. What should this safe point be?
    Some ideas are for the ego car to resume accepting and applying positive acceleration
    after:

    ∙ a certain duration has elapsed, or

        51

   ∙ the ego car has moved a certain distance, or

   ∙ a certain separation distance has been established between the ego and lead
      cars, or

   ∙ the ego car’s velocity has decreased to a certain velocity, or

   ∙ some combination thereof.

   Considering the first option, we argue that we care about duration ultimately
because that translates to the change in the displacement of the ego car and the
resultant change in the separation distance, if the ego car continues accelerating in
that period of time. We know from the kinematics equations at the beginning of
Chapter 2 that this displacement is heavily dependent on the actual velocity and
acceleration of the ego car.
   So we skip to the second-to-last option that considers velocity. How do we de-
termine a safe point value that the ego car’s velocity should decrease to? We reason
that this has to be related in some way to the velocities of the ego and/or lead car,
or perhaps their relative velocities, at the moment the Interlock is triggered or poten-
tially some time before. If the ego car is traveling at 30 𝑚/𝑠 when it violates the safe
separation distance, we probably want it to slow down to a different safe point value
than if it were traveling at 5 𝑚/𝑠 when it violates the safe separation distance. We
imagine that we will want Interlock to decelerate the car (and block it from acceler-
ating) for a longer duration and to block the car from accelerating in that time if the
car’s velocity was higher at the moment it violated safe separation distance, or if the
acceleration input that we are blocking it from is higher. A feasible initial choice for
the safe point velocity is some value that is proportional to the velocity of the ego
car at the moment Interlock is triggered, so we present our exploration of that in the
following section.
   Since jittering is the phenomenon of the ego car repeatedly violating and re-
establishing safe separation, the option of blocking the ego car from accelerating until
a certain separation distance has been regained also looks promising. This may be the

        52

simplest option to test in our simulator: as with the choice of the safe point velocity,
we choose the safe point separation distance to be proportional to the safe separation
distance at the moment it is violated.
   To explore how we can define a safe point based on the re-establishment of a
certain separation distance and/or the reduction of the ego car’s velocity to some
value, we implemented a method in the simulator that allowed us to experiment with
different proportional values and observe the car’s resulting behavior. We discuss this
next.

the car is currently very close to the border of the safe separation distance. So we
first augment the interlock() method in the Car class to track whether the Inter-

intervention_sep and intervention_vel respectively. Finally, we add sep_mult and
vel_mult, proportional variables that we can tune to set the safe point separation
distance and/or velocity to multiples of (respectively) the safe separation distance
and/or the ego car’s velocity at last Interlock intervention. To summarize, we aug-

We have presented an approach of defining a safe point using the separation distance
and the ego car’s velocity at the moment of Interlock intervention, such that jittering
is prevented by blocking any acceleration and continuing to apply maximum decel-

include:

   ∙ defining the safe point using other parameters listed in Section 4.1

   ∙ exploring those parameters but not at the moment of Interlock intervention

   ∙ maintaining the ego car’s acceleration at zero instead of continuing to apply
      maximum deceleration until it reaches the safe point

   Going further, there are almost certainly other ways of preventing jittering beyond
our discussion of safe points and those listed parameters; this could be an area to look
into for further fruitful exploration.

5.1.2 Applications

In the single lane scenario, the car has no choice but to slow down to avoid a collision,
but when there are more lanes the situation quickly becomes more interesting: is it
safer for the ego car to slow down, or to change lanes? If the car has alternatives
besides slowing down, we may be able to relax the bounds on the minimum safe
separation distance. At the same time, lane changing introduces the risk of colliding
with cars in the other lanes, especially a car that is currently behind the ego car in an

        61

adjacent lane, and speeds up just as the ego car is changing to this adjacent lane. The
Interlock will have to compute and deal with these risks if we extend the simulator
to modeling multi-lane road scenarios.

5.1.3 Improvements

The PID controller implemented by the RailroadCar to change lanes currently uses
a P value of 0.05 and a D value of 3 (and does not use the integral term at all), which
produces a smooth lane change for a RailroadCar traveling at 30 𝑚/𝑠 as in our
demo in Figure 5-1. However, those PID values when combined with other velocities
can produce unsteady swerving behavior, as shown in Figure 5-3. It may be worth
looking further into this or exploring alternative methods of modeling lane changing.
Alternatively, there might be use cases for simulating odd swerving behavior, for
instance in a car whose driver has lost control of it. The following section discusses
recording arbitrary trajectories in the simulator to replay in later simulations, so it
is possible to simulate an out-of-control car using other values for the PID controller
and reusing this car in the simulator.

that timestep. This enables the non-ego car in this new simulation to replicate the
exact trajectory of the car we controlled in the first simulation. Any initial velocity
and acceleration values passed to this car through the command line arguments will
be ignored.
   The Interlock algorithm for the ‘single lane, two cars’ scenario will cause the ego
car to slow down when it gets too close to the decelerating lead car, and stop before
colliding with the lead car. We can record and replay more complicated trajectories
such as a car repeatedly speeding up and slowing down, or even a car changing lanes
on a multi-lane road. In the simulation shown in Figure 5-6, the lead car (in red) is
replaying a trajectory from a previous simulation in which we changed lanes multiple
times. This results in a collision when the lead car veers back into the path of the
ego car (in green), since the Interlock in our simulator is currently equipped only to
deal with the ‘single lane, two cars’ scenario.

5.2.1 Applications

There is great value in being able to simulate any trajectory to test how the ego car
responds. This is perhaps less evident in the ‘single lane, two cars’ scenario, where we
can already simulate a variety of scenarios involving different combinations of initial
velocities and accelerations in the ego and lead cars just from the command line. But
even for that scenario, we just saw demos in which users have finer control over the
trajectory of the lead car, and can then replay that trajectory and confirm that the
Interlock keeps the ego car safe in reaction to any user-generated trajectory in the
lead car.
   If we want to simulate more complicated patterns of acceleration/deceleration in
the lead car for this scenario, this feature enables us to intuitively generate the pattern
simply by pressing keys to control the car, and to do this only once and then be able
to replay the exact trajectory any number of times in future simulations. We can
build a library of “templates” of common trajectories that we are likely to frequently
model.
   Beyond the ‘single lane, two cars’ scenario, more complicated scenarios may ne-
cessitate using this feature to model how other cars may behave. For instance, if we
want to verify an Interlock algorithm that enables the ego car to safely react to a
car in an adjacent lane that suddenly switches to its lane, an option is to create the
lane-changing trajectory in a previous simulation and program the other car with this

        66

trajectory when launching this simulation. Another option is to design the simulator
to ingest user input to control the other car while the animation is running— but
what happens if there are multiple other cars? This alternative may quickly become
unwieldy if we want to extend the simulator to scenarios with multiple other cars: it
will be difficult for us to design and implement, and it may require the user to follow
more specifications to select which of the other cars they are trying to control.
   With this record-and-replay functionality, however, there is virtually no limit to
how creative we can get with the trajectories of all of the other cars in a simulation.
We can record a variety of trajectories beforehand, and when we launch the current
simulation, read all these trajectories from their respective files for the other cars
that we want to create; additionally, we can still initialize any car in any lane and at
any offset from the start of the lane— so the trajectory need not begin at the same
place as it started from when we recorded it. Furthermore, the other cars can be a
mix of replays of previously recorded trajectories as well as standard RailroadCars
initialized with a user-specificed velocity and acceleration.

5.3.2 Improvements

We have mentioned curved roads being a major area of improvement to realize the
full potential of the simulator in modeling a variety of road scenarios. We reiterate
this point: many roads in the real world have some curvature, including most actual
entrance and exit ramps onto highways. Curved road configurations like roundabouts
and winding mountain roads are currently impossible to create in the current simu-
lator. Implementing the functionality to create curved roads would also require im-
plementing lane changing on curved roads, either in the current RailroadCar class,
or in a new subclass of the Car class (or even in a new subclass of the RailroadCar
subclass).
   We have also pointed out that the inability of RailroadCars to move between
Roads is a major limitation. Visually the simulator is already capable of modeling
scenarios involving multiple roads whose position and orientation the user is able to
customize, but being able to actually have cars drive between roads would enable the
simulator to functionally model more diverse scenarios such as a car making a left
turn at a T-junction.

    Chapter 6

    Conclusion



A new design architecture for self-driving cars, in which a small, trusted, indepen-
dent module of code interacts with the main controller to keep the car safe, has the
potential to enable far easier safety verification. The module of code, which we term
the Interlock, can be independently verified using formal verification techniques as
well as virtual and physical simulations. This is much more feasible than evaluating
the entire complex system of a self-driving car using a statistical approach, which
requires self-driving cars to have driven on the order of billions of miles to provide a
convincing safety argument.
   We have presented our evaluation of the ‘single lane, two cars’ scenario, and used
formal verification to prove safety conditions that enable a self-driving car to stop in
time to avoid collision with another car ahead if the latter suddenly brakes. We fully
defined a safe separation distance for the ego car, based on all possible combinations
of velocity and acceleration/deceleration properties of the self-driving car (the ‘ego’
car) and the car ahead (the ‘lead’ car), accounting also for the cycle time of the
Interlock and sensor and actuator delays.
   We also developed a virtual simulator using the Processing programming language
to provide further visual evidence that the Interlock keeps the car safe. While the
simulator was intended to double-check the safety conditions verified by formal tech-
niques, on top of that it actually revealed a problem in the ‘single lane, two cars’
scenario that was not evident through formal verification. This issue—which we term

        73

jittering—arises because the ego car repeatedly slows down upon violating the safe
separation distance, and then speeds up when it has just regained sufficient distance,
only to promptly violate the safe separation distance again.
   Jittering is an interesting problem to which we have envisioned multiple angles of
approach; we have discussed these ideas and verified in our simulator that some of
these ideas indeed prevent jittering. It is our hope that our work in Section 4.2 will
provide a starting point to demonstrate how the simulator code can be modified for
further experiments on resolving the jittering issue.
   Because this simulator began with the goal of verifying safety in a range of road
scenarios, it was designed to be highly customizable. We have shown how it may be
extended beyond the ‘single lane, two cars’ scenario to model lane changing, arbitrary
trajectories, and diverse road configurations.


6.1 Improvements and Future Work

At the same time, we have brought up limitations of the simulator, or they might
have become apparent during the discussions in Chapter 3 and 5. Instead of simply
rehashing these limitations, we make a couple of general remarks about overarching
considerations in the pros and cons of the simulator’s design:

   ∙ We have demonstrated the versatility of Processing in processing user input
      from the command line, but this requires the user to download the Processing
      program and the simulator code to their local machine in order to launch the
      simulation from their terminal. This comes at a cost in terms of the portability
      offered by Processing.js, which enables code written in Processing to run in
      most browsers, so the user does not need to download any software—but she
      also cannot interact with a Processing.js simulation from the command line.
      This is a trade-off that must be made between the power of Processing and the
      convenience of Processing.js. A workaround is to create UI/UX components
      in the window to ingest user input before starting the simulation of the road
      scenario, which leads us to the next point.

        74

   ∙ There is arguably no limit to how customizable we can make the simulator, in
      terms of user input to initialize the road scenario, user input while the animation
      is running, the degree of control the user has over every property of a road or
      a car or any other class we may create, etc. We can also get wildly creative
      with creating visual feedback for the user and adding UI/UX components in
      the window to ingest user input. But the trade-off is that the code will quickly
      become increasingly complex and difficult to maintain, so it is important to
      consider how customizable a property really needs to be.

   In light of making the simulator extensible to diverse road scenarios, we propose
these immediate next steps to implement in the simulator:

   ∙ curved roads,

   ∙ modeling cars driving between roads, and

   ∙ a scrolling feature: have the roads “scroll” in some way in the simulator so that
      we can continue following the cars once they reach the end of the road (currently
      they simply drive off-screen which does not work for longer trajectories).


6.2 Tying Back to the Broader Context

The goal of the Interlock is to be applicable to more road scenarios than the ‘sin-
gle lane, two cars’ case, but establishing what constitutes a safe separation distance
in that case is a critical foundation block on which to build more complex scenar-
ios, and we have rigorously verified it here through both formal techniques and the
development of a simulation program.
   We have also raised the question of whether preventing collision is necessarily
the most important safety concern. We formally verified the safe separation distance
using the most conservative assumption of a “worst” possible lead car—one which
applies maximum deceleration—and under the condition that the ego car also applies
maximum deceleration. What if a more comfortable braking, with weaker deceler-
ation, could actually be safer? Or what if we are willing to tolerate some risk of

        75

collision in order to get to our destination faster, especially if the actual probability
of collision is low? For instance, Pierson et al. [22] described the idea of “risk level
sets”: using a cost function that accounts for the density and motion of other cars on
the road, the ego car plans its trajectory from the set of allowed maneuvers around
the other cars, which is based on the level of risk that it is willing to accept. So an ego
car with higher risk tolerance would change lanes more frequently instead of slowing
down in its lane to follow the car ahead, despite the higher risk of a collision when
changing lanes in dense traffic.
   The Interlock’s current strategy of braking with maximum deceleration if the safe
separation distance is violated is one algorithm that we have proven works, but it is
just one possible strategy after all. It does not exclude consideration of other strategies
for the same scenario, and it is likely that we will incorporate other dimensions into
our consideration of “safety” as the project progresses, and this will challenge what
the simulator and the ‘single lane, two cars’ analysis discussed in this thesis currently
offer.
   More generally, the simulator will likely need to be continually redesigned as the
Interlock evolves. For instance, it is not readily extensible to a scenario in which
multiple cars are running the Interlock—which is a potential design for the Interlock
that we have discussed. In the context of the larger project, we should remember that
the simulator is meant to serve as one of many verification tools, so as we broaden
discussion of how the Interlock should handle different road scenarios, the simulator
should first be evaluated for how suitable it is for modeling a particular Interlock
algorithm for a particular road scenario, before we dive into any update or revamp of
its design.
